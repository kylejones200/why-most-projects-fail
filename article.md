### Why (most) Projects Fail
Most projects fail because leaders mistake activity for progress.
Meetings pile up, task lists grow, and dashboards glow with color. But
deadlines slip, budgets swell, and the finish line drifts out of reach.

Industry data makes the picture plain. PMI reports that 70 percent of
projects miss their objectives. That number repeats across sectors,
geographies, and tools. You can adopt Agile, waterfall, or hybrids with
new names. The outcome does not change.

I saw this firsthand with a large utility. The team managed its work
across three project tracking platforms. Status updates came like
clockwork. Charts filled binders. Yet the real obstacle sat in the open.
No executive sponsor claimed the project. Priorities shifted with each
quarter, and the project floated without defense. It failed, not from
lack of structure, but from lack of ownership.

Tools and checklists can hide the core problem. A project without
support at the top will drift no matter how much tracking takes place.
Leaders confuse motion with traction. They measure effort instead of
accountability.

The fix is simple, though not easy. Leaders should cut noise and ask one
question: who owns the outcome? Not who manages the tasks. Not who
tracks the budget. Who will defend the project when another executive
demands resources or when the board questions the spend? If no one holds
that role, the project is already broken.

Projects do not fail for lack of tracking. They fail for lack of
ownership. Until leaders solve that problem, the numbers will not move.
